# Layout Composition Rules

Use this file when the user wants a page that feels premium, editorial, futuristic, campaign-like, luxury, hardware-forward, or unusually art directed.

This resource exists to stop prompts from collapsing into "clean components in stacked sections."

## Core Principle

High-end landing pages are usually defined by composition before components.

That means the prompt should establish:

1. What dominates the frame.
2. Where the negative space lives.
3. How typography and imagery compete or cooperate.
4. Which materials are primary, secondary, and tertiary.
5. How the page breathes from section to section.

## Visual Thesis

Before section specs, define the page in one sentence:

- `product-as-sculpture in a dark optical field`
- `editorial asymmetry with sparse luxury spacing`
- `cinematic industrial layout with directional tension`
- `museum-like technical minimalism`
- `campaign-style fashion tech composition with floating annotations`

If the output lacks a clear thesis, it will usually degrade into a generic product page.

## Composition Rules

Prefer these concepts when the product calls for premium presentation:

- `editorial asymmetry`
- `negative-space-led layout`
- `offset composition`
- `anchored text block`
- `cropped cinematic framing`
- `top-weighted hero`
- `bottom-anchored product stage`
- `immersive full-bleed moment`
- `restrained information clustering`

Describe composition in directional terms, not just grid terms.

Instead of:

- `split layout with image left and text right`

Prefer:

- `an asymmetrical composition where the product dominates roughly 65% of the frame and the text is anchored into the remaining negative space`

## Hero Rules

Every hero should have one dominant visual force:

1. The product object.
2. The headline block.
3. The environmental media.
4. A floating data/widget surface.

Do not let all four fight equally.

For high-end hardware, avoid describing the product as a normal image card.
Prefer language such as:

- `suspended in a black optical environment`
- `resting on a reflective pedestal`
- `treated like a campaign object, not an e-commerce thumbnail`
- `lit with controlled edge glow and sparse reflections`

## Negative Space And Rhythm

Premium pages rarely use uniform spacing from top to bottom.

Use deliberate variation:

- one section can feel sparse and slow
- the next can feel dense and technical
- a later section can reset with an immersive full-bleed image or video

Useful language:

- `sparse luxury spacing`
- `compressed technical strip`
- `oversized pause before CTA`
- `long visual exhale between headline and support copy`
- `non-uniform section pacing`

## Typography Contrast Rules

Premium layout depends on contrast, not just font selection.

Specify:

1. Which text is quiet and which is theatrical.
2. Where oversized type is allowed.
3. Where microcopy should behave like labels or instrumentation.
4. Whether the headline should feel literary, industrial, optical, or corporate.

Useful pairings:

- serif italics for the emotional or visionary phrase
- disciplined sans for UI and support text
- monospace for technical specs, labels, or telemetry

Useful language:

- `museum-label typography`
- `tight tracking on the headline, spacious tracking on labels`
- `oversized display paired with restrained body text`
- `small technical notations around a large emotional headline`

## Material Hierarchy

Do not give every effect equal weight.

Always define:

1. Primary material: what dominates most surfaces.
2. Secondary material: what adds emphasis.
3. Tertiary material: what appears sparingly.

Examples:

- primary: matte black shells and soft lens reflections
- secondary: translucent smoked glass surfaces
- tertiary: cyan optical glow only around lenses, separators, or active UI

Or:

- primary: soft white field with near-invisible borders
- secondary: warm metallic accent
- tertiary: thin motion lines and restrained blur

This prevents prompts from overusing glow, gradients, glass, blur, chrome, and particles all at once.

## Section Rhythm

For full pages, decide what each section is doing in the overall experience:

1. Hero: attraction
2. Secondary block: explanation
3. Immersive media block: emotional proof
4. Technical strip or metrics: authority
5. Testimonial or use case: trust
6. CTA: closure

Not every page needs every section, but each included section should have a role.

## Hardware / Future Product Guidance

When working on premium hardware, AR/VR, automotive, robotics, audio, or futuristic consumer electronics:

- treat the product as sculpture
- use optical language rather than app language
- keep UI overlays sparse and intentional
- use annotation layers only when they reinforce the fantasy
- prefer reflective control over broad neon glow
- make supporting modules feel like instrumentation, not dashboard cards

## Editorial / Brand Campaign Guidance

When the mood is fashion, editorial, climate, premium consulting, or design-forward agency:

- favor asymmetry over equal columns
- use strong type-image tension
- let one phrase carry emotional weight
- keep cards restrained and avoid over-populating the page
- allow generous dark areas or quiet white areas to carry the brand mood

## Motion Tone

For premium pages, motion should feel optical, cinematic, and deliberate.

Prefer:

- slow reveal
- long easing tails
- subtle parallax
- stagger with restraint
- motion tied to depth, not gimmicks

Avoid:

- bouncy UI motion
- loud spring animations
- every card animating aggressively
- motion that feels like a dashboard product demo unless the page is explicitly SaaS-first

## Prompting Checklist

Before finalizing a prompt, verify:

1. Is there a one-line visual thesis?
2. Does the hero have one dominant visual force?
3. Is composition described beyond simple columns?
4. Is negative space intentional?
5. Is material hierarchy clearly stated?
6. Does the page have changing rhythm across sections?
7. Does typography define hierarchy, not just font names?
8. Does motion match the brand tone?
